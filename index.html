One generalist robot model, many bodies, no fine‑tuning.

π0.7 is Physical Intelligence's latest vision‑language‑action model. It folds a t‑shirt on a robot it was never trained on, operates an unfamiliar air fryer with the help of a few sentences, and matches per‑task RL specialists across laundry, espresso, and box assembly — all from the same set of weights.

This page walks you from "what is a robot policy?" all the way to the trick that makes π0.7 work: diverse contextual prompting. Every concept gets its own live widget. No robotics background needed.

§0.1 · A robot policy, in one widget

A policy is a function. It turns what the robot sees into what the robot does.

Pick up your coffee. Your eyes catch the mug; a millisecond later your shoulder, elbow and wrist start rotating. There is something — a "policy" — mapping what you see to how you move.

For a robot the inputs are camera pixels and joint angles. The outputs are target joint angles a few hundred milliseconds into the future. The policy is the function in between.

Drag the sliders to set this toy two‑joint arm's angles directly. You're being the policy.

A robot policy is a learned function $\pi(a \mid o)$: given an observation $o$ (pixels + joint angles), produce an action $a$ (next joint angles).

§0.2 · Why this is harder than it looks

For one observation there are many valid actions.

Reach for your coffee from above; from the side; via a little curl. All three are correct. Robot data is full of this: human demonstrators picked their own paths, and the "right action" given the same image is genuinely multimodal.

This is why naive supervised learning struggles. If you fit a model that emits one action per observation by minimizing squared error, it averages the modes — and the average is often somewhere none of the demonstrators went.

Action distributions are multimodal. A policy that collapses them to a mean produces motion that is the average of two good plans, which is usually a bad plan.

Three different joint paths reach the same yellow target. A model that averaged them would pass through none of them.

§0.3 · Imitation learning, in one chart

Show the robot enough demos and a policy emerges. Mostly.

Behavior cloning is the simplest recipe. Record a human teleoperating the robot, store every (observation, action) pair, then train a network to predict the action from the observation.

Click on the canvas to add demonstrations. Each click is an (observation, action) pair: x is the observation, y is the action the demonstrator chose. The dashed curve is what a small model learns. The shaded band is its coverage — regions where it's seen enough nearby demos to be confident.

Watch what happens at the edges where you haven't clicked. The coverage thins, the curve gets nervous. That gap is where π0.7 has to do real work.

Imitation learning fits the demonstrators well in regions you've covered. Out‑of‑distribution observations are the hard part — and there are always more of those than you can demonstrate.

§0.5 · From action tokens to trajectories

π‑family models predict continuous action chunks via flow matching.

If you only emit one action at a time, the robot stutters; chunks of actions look smoother and let the model commit to a plan. But chunks live in a high‑dimensional continuous space, and we already saw that supervised regression averages modes.

Flow matchingA way to train a generative model: start from random noise and learn a vector field that pushes noise toward data. At inference, you integrate that field over time $t \in [0,1]$ to sample. Closely related to diffusion, but with a simpler objective.
sidesteps that. Instead of predicting an action, the model learns a velocity field on action‑space. At inference, you start from a Gaussian blob and let the field carry you to a sample. Multiple modes? The field has multiple basins. No averaging.

Drag the slider for $t$. At $t=0$ you see noise. At $t=1$ the noise has been transported onto the data distribution. Toggle the number of modes to see the field reorganize.

Flow matching turns "predict an action" into "follow a learned velocity field from noise to data." Multimodal distributions are first‑class citizens.

§1 · The thesis

One model. Many robot bodies. No fine‑tuning.

π0.7's headline claim is generalization across both tasks and embodiments: a single set of weights that performs new manipulation skills and runs on robots whose hardware it has never directly trained on.

The video on the right is the showcase: a t‑shirt folded by a bimanual UR5e — an industrial arm that contributed zero training data. PI reports the success rate matches what their expert teleoperators achieve when handed the same robot for the first time. (See the blog post for details.)

This is a step‑change. Earlier π‑family models — including π0.6 — were largely specialists: each task got its own RL fine‑tune. π0.7 keeps that quality but emits it from one generalist.

π0.7 is a generalist. The same checkpoint folds laundry on a new robot, makes espresso, peels vegetables, and operates an unfamiliar air fryer. The rest of the page is about how.

§2 · The naive recipe — and why it fails

Just dump every robot's data into one model? Try it.

The obvious recipe for a generalist: collect demonstrations from every robot you can, mix all the data together, and train one big network. PI tried it. It does not work — and the reason is the same multimodality problem from §0.2, just worse.

Two robots see the same image and produce different correct actions, because their bodies are different. A wide‑gripper bimanual arm folds a shirt one way; a narrow single‑gripper does it differently. To a model with no idea which body it's controlling, this looks like one robot doing contradictory things.

Below: same observation $x$, two demonstrators producing different correct actions. Train a single‑mode regressor on the mix and it averages them — the dashed line. Toggle "add a label distinguishing them" and the model can finally fit both. That toggle is the entire intuition for §3.

Without a label saying which robot or how the task should be done, multi‑robot data looks self‑contradictory. The fix is to hand the model that label as a conditioning signal.

Same observation, two demonstrators

Off: the model sees one mixed dataset and averages. On: it conditions on a label and fits both modes cleanly.

§3 · Diverse contextual prompting

Tell the model not just what to do, but how it's being done.

The fix in §2 — adding a "which robot" label — is the seed of π0.7's central technique. Instead of a single label, π0.7 conditions every training example on a structured bundle of context:

  • Language — a high‑level task and finer sub‑step instructions ("open the air fryer", "grasp the lid handle").
  • Metadata — performance attributes like quality, speed, or which strategy the demonstrator used.
  • Control modality — joint‑space vs. end‑effector targets; gripper open/close abstractions.
  • Visual subgoals — an image of what the scene should look like a few hundred milliseconds from now.

Toggle the chips on the right. Each combination corresponds to a slice of the training data the model would see, and changes what conditioning the policy gets at inference.

Diverse contextual prompting disambiguates conflicting demonstrations and lets a single network absorb data from many robots, many strategies, even human videos — without averaging them away.

§4 · The world model loop

A small model paints what should happen next. The policy aims at the painting.

Among the four conditioning channels in §3, visual subgoals deserve their own section. Demonstrators rarely write down their plan; they just act. So PI trains a lightweight world model that, given the current frame plus a high‑level instruction, generates an image of the desired next state. The action policy is then conditioned on that synthetic image at inference.

Step through the loop on the right: observation → world‑model subgoal → action chunk → next observation. The frames are real captures from PI's blog post.

Why this helps: subgoals factor planning ("what should the scene look like in 0.5 s?") out of the action policy ("what joint motion realizes that?"). Each piece does what it's good at.

Visual subgoals turn an abstract instruction into a concrete pixel target. The action policy learns a much easier mapping: from this image to this image, what motion?

§5 · Compositional generalization

Skills the model never learned together — recombined to solve new tasks.

Once the model can absorb diverse data through diverse prompts, something interesting happens. Skills it learned in isolation — closing a drawer, pressing a button, opening a hinged lid — start recombining for tasks no single demonstration covered. PI's headline example is operating an air fryer the model never saw, by composing motions from training on toasters, microwaves, and other appliances.

Drag skill cards into the target slot and watch the corresponding capability tier light up. Then play the matching video to see what π0.7 actually does on the real machine.

This is a language‑model‑style emergent capability — the same way LLMs that learned French and JSON can suddenly produce French JSON without ever being trained on the combination.

π0.7 shows the first signs of compositional generalization in robotics: not just "do task X better", but "combine skills A and B for the unseen task C".

§6 · Specialist‑level performance, in one generalist

Distill RL specialists into the generalist with strategy metadata.

The conventional way to get high task performance is to fine‑tune a per‑task specialist with RL. PI did exactly that — and then poured the resulting behavior back into π0.7, tagging each example with metadata describing the strategy the specialist used. The generalist matches or beats the specialists on the tasks PI evaluates.

Specialists are no longer the ceiling. With diverse contextual prompting, the generalist matches them and keeps every other skill it had.

§7 · What's still missing

An honest scorecard.

PI is candid that π0.7 is the beginning, not the end. Compositional generalization shows up in early forms; physical generalization (new objects, surfaces, lighting) lags semantic generalization (new instructions, new task labels); and explicit "think‑then‑act" planning isn't really there yet.

The bars on the right are an editorial summary, not a benchmark — read the paper's discussion section for the precise picture. Future versions of π‑family models will likely close most of these.

π0.7 is a step‑change, not a finish line. Reasoning about plans, recovering from outcomes, and generalizing physically are the next problems.
